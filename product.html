Phase 1 — Listen

SME Interviews: EdTech in U.S. vs. China

I interviewed subject-matter experts to compare how EdTech products are built and adopted across the U.S. and China. The conversations revealed how cultural context, regulatory environments, and teacher expectations shape fundamentally different product requirements.

Key Insight

The same tutoring concept requires entirely different trust models — U.S. educators want transparency and opt-in control, while Chinese educators prioritize measurable outcome gains.

Phase 2 — Synthesize

SME Interview: Special Education in the U.S.

I studied extreme use cases in special education to stress-test product assumptions. Interviewing practitioners revealed how designing for edge cases — sensory needs, nonverbal communication, variable attention — produces more resilient products for everyone.

Key Insight

Products designed for the margins don't just serve niche users — they surface durability requirements that mainstream design overlooks until it's too late.

Phase 3 — Define

TurtleTalk — Problem Framing & Architecture

TurtleTalk is a voice-chat companion that helps children reframe hard moments and build deeper connection with their parents — kids talk to Shelly the sea turtle, get warm voice replies, and earn brave missions that turn into weekly conversation starters at the dinner table.

Applied the Jobs-to-Be-Done framework and value proposition canvas to define what parents and children actually need — not features, but outcomes. This phase translated interview findings into a clear product-market fit hypothesis around emotional support and parent-child connection.

Key Insight

Parents don't hire a chat app to "entertain their kid" — they hire it to give their child a safe place to process big feelings, and a bridge back to talking with them.

View JTBD & Value Prop →

Designed the system architecture: how the voice pipeline, AI conversation engine, mission system, and parent dashboard interact. Made deliberate trade-offs between real-time responsiveness and emotional safety, documented in component diagrams and data flow maps.

Key Insight

The hardest architectural decision wasn't technical — it was deciding where the AI should stop generating freely and defer to scripted responses, to keep the experience emotionally safe for kids.

Phase 4 — Build

TurtleTalk — Shipping the Prototype

Shipped a working prototype during a 4-week sprint at Founders, Inc. Built on Next.js + TypeScript with a swappable voice pipeline — kids speak to Shelly the sea turtle (plan to render in 3D powered by Koyal.ai), the audio is transcribed and routed through an LLM, and the reply is voiced back in real time.

Stack & Decisions

Frontend: Next.js, TypeScript, React Three Fiber for the 3D Shelly.   Voice: pluggable providers — native browser VAD, Vapi, Gemini Live, and LiveKit — so we could A/B latency and naturalness without rewriting the app.   LLM: Anthropic Claude / OpenAI / Google Gemini routed through LangChain.   TTS: ElevenLabs for warm, child-friendly voices.   Backend: Supabase for child auth, missions, weekly reports, and the parent dashboard.   Deploy: Vercel.
